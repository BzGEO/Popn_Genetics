# Reflections on Belizean History
*Last updated 🕚 03.07.2026*

***What I did not learn at SJC***
* From August 1993 through June 1997, I attended [St. John's College](https://www.sjc.edu.bz/) in Belize City, founded in 1887 and the third oldest secondary school in Belize (and generally considered one of the best, if not *the* best secondary school in the country). I took history classes throughout my four years there, and I was usually one of the top students, as evidenced by the honors certificates I have kept. And while I got a very good education in Belizean and West Indian history, and while my teachers were undoubtedly some of the best in the country, we were taught Belizean history in particular in a very disconnected sense. For instance, we learned about the colonial settlement in the 1600s from before Belize was even called British Honduras, but **we were not taught that we were descended from the the people participating in that history**.

***Learning about my personal connection to the past***
* In August 2014, I visited the national archives (formally, the [Belize Archives and Records Service](https://archives.gov.bz/)) in Belmopan for the first time. It wasn't until my visit there that I was able to connect my own existence with the existence of the Belizeans whose actions reverberated through our history. For instance, I learned that through my Cherrington line, I am descended from one of the ['Flowers Bank Fourteen'](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=W56NZIToHsM) whose votes at a public meeting in June 1797 were decisive in the settlers deciding to fight an eventual planned Spanish invasion in September of 1798 (for which the 10th of September holiday is celebrated). I would certainly have appreciated been cognizant of that while I was a teenager and not until I was nearing my mid-30s, as it would have given me a very different appreciation of Belizean history.
* That is, I think many Belizeans view our country's history with a kind of sterile appreciation for those who came before us, rather than that we carry the blood of the people who made our country what it is. We carry their DNA, and they live on in us.

***Connecting the Flowers of 1797 with the Flowers of the present day***
* Also in 2014, by way of an auspicious circumstance, I also came upon some interesting [otherwise privileged] demographic data for contemporary Belize. What that data showed was that a significant proportion of Belizeans carried the last name Flowers, and were therefore also likely linked to the handful of siblings who originally established Flowers Bank following the arrival of people from Britain's former [Mosquito Shore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mosquito_Coast) colony on the Caribbean coasts of Honduras and Nicaragua.
* Of course, connections to colonial era settlers aren't the only connections we can establish, although those are connections which have paper trails that can be investigated at our national archives. Belizeans who have done genetic testing via companies like 23andMe, Ancestry.com, and Family Tree DNA, among others, can document genetic connections to peoples which can supplement oral histories.

***A revised history curriculum at SJC***
* In Nov. 2019, while visiting with a friend (Kirkland Smith) who was teaching at SJC, I had the opportunity to chat with Yasser Musa and Delmer Tzib, who had apparently reinvigorated the Belizean History curriculum at SJC, and who had set up a website (now defunct) with different materials on Belizean history. I spoke with Yasser and Delmer about the findings from the genetic research I had engaged in, and I likewise conveyed that I had not learned history at SJC in a more active form.
